Does a simulated organoid actually compute?

A reproducibility check on the memory-capacity study in organoid_findings_report.pdf — re-run from the source code in this repo, reporting what the data supports and what it doesn't.

What this study is. Separate from the interactive creature, the organoid_simulator/ package models organoid-like tissue as a sparse recurrent network under Dale's Law (80% excitatory / 20% inhibitory) and measures two things as the network grows from 100 to 200,000 neurons: memory capacity (how many past inputs a linear readout can reconstruct) and criticality (whether activity sits near the "edge of chaos"). It's a reservoir-computing benchmark, not a claim about biological cognition.

1. Is the machinery real, or hand-tuned?

The strongest evidence that this is genuine computation rather than a rigged demo is the linear-shift-register control. I fed the memory-capacity estimator a "reservoir" that is literally a 10-tap delay line — its state at time t is exactly the last 10 inputs. A correct estimator must return MC ≈ 10.0 and no more. Running the repo's own compute_memory_capacity() unchanged:

MC = 10.07 on the 10-tap control (expected ≈ 10.0). The ridge-regression readout — including the dual-form solver used for the huge networks — recovers exactly the recoverable information and nothing spurious. The measurement is trustworthy.

I also rebuilt three reservoirs from scratch (build_recurrent_weights → scale_spectral_radius → run_homeostasis → run_simulation) and re-measured MC live, independent of the stored results:

The real headline: MC grows logarithmically with N. A single log fit — MC = 3.57 + 0.57·log₁₀N — tracks the data cleanly across 3.5 orders of magnitude (predicting 4.7 at N=100 and 6.4 at N=100k). Two robust, honest effects fall out:
  • Bigger reservoirs remember more, but with sharply diminishing returns — a 2000× increase in neurons buys only ~1.5 extra units of capacity.
  • Sparser is better here: K=50 beats K=100 at every size. Fewer, stronger connections preserve more of the input history than denser wiring under this normalization.

3. Where the PDF oversells itself

The write-up is worth trusting on method but not on every adjective. Two claims in the PDF are not supported by the numbers the same PDF reports:

Claim: a sharp capability "knee" — "MC < 1.5 below N=1,000." not supported
The data never dips near 1.5. MC is already 4.45 at N=100, the smallest network tested. There is no knee; there is a gentle logarithmic climb. The "localized activations that soon decay" story doesn't appear in these runs.

Claim: avalanche exponent "τ ≈ 1.5, aligning with mean-field theory." not supported
Across the sweep τ clusters at 2.2–2.5, not 1.5. The only run near 1.5 is N=200,000 (τ≈1.17) — and that fit has xmin=2 with a near-zero p-value, i.e. a degenerate fit over tiny avalanches, not evidence of clean criticality. Similarly, the branching ratio sits subcritical at ~0.85 for most sizes, not "≈0.98 / edge-of-chaos." The network is stable and slightly damped, which is fine — just not critical.

What the criticality data does support

The one criticality claim that holds up is directional: memory capacity peaks near ρ ≈ 0.95, the classic echo-state "edge of chaos." My live N=300 sweep confirms the ordering, though the effect is modest:

4. Honest verdict on realism

Realistic as a reservoir-computing model? Yes. The dynamics, the Dale's-Law sign constraint, the spectral-radius normalization, and the memory-capacity measurement are all textbook-correct and independently reproduce. The shift-register control proves the numbers aren't inflated.

Realistic as a model of a biological organoid? Only loosely. This shares the honesty of the interactive creature: the structure is biologically flavored, the quantities are not calibrated. Specifically:

  • Rate units, not spikes. Neurons here are tanh rate units with a leak term — not the Izhikevich spiking cells of the interactive sim, and not real membrane dynamics. "Avalanches" are thresholded excursions of a rate signal, a proxy for the spiking cascades measured on real MEAs.
  • Static homeostasis. Activity is centered by an offline binary search on input gain, done once — not the ongoing synaptic scaling a living culture runs continuously.
  • No plasticity or learning in the reservoir. Weights are fixed after construction; only the linear readout is trained. Nothing self-organizes. This is standard for reservoir computing, but it means the "organoid" doesn't adapt.
  • Scale gap remains vast. 200k rate units is a large simulation but a small organoid, and connectivity (K=50–100 per neuron) is far below biological fan-out.
  • The plateau is a ceiling artifact. MC is bounded by k_max=50 and the training length; the flattening at large N is partly the measurement's own limit, which the PDF correctly flags.
Bottom line. The code does what it says and the results are reproducible — I re-ran them. The defensible finding is logarithmic memory scaling with size, sparser-is-better connectivity, and a mild edge-of-chaos optimum. The "sharp knee" and "τ≈1.5 criticality" language in the PDF is stronger than its own data, and should be read as aspiration rather than result.
